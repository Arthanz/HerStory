Is Her Story a Delusion or Deception? The Case of Hannah Smith’s Fractured Mind



LONDON (ANB NEWS) – Police investigators have reached a verdict that diagnoses Hannah with Multiple Personality Disorder (MPD), where her secondary personality, Eve, eventually takes over, after a comprehensive analysis of complex mental tests and a careful scrutiny of diaries that reveal the disjointed state of Hannah and Eve's mind.

Previously referred to as Multiple Personality Disorder, Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID) is defined by having two or more distinct personalities that periodically assume control over a victim's body, often leading to memory gaps and contradictions. Forensic psychologists working on the case have confirmed that Hannah's condition fits into the category for DID, explaining for themselves the discrepancies in her testimony, physical appearance, and actions.

The Conclusion and the Evidence
Following a thorough analysis of the evidence, law enforcement authorities, in conjunction with specialist psychologists, came to a shocking realization. Hannah and Eve were not separate entities but creations of a singularly divided mind. The theory is strongly supported by the evidence, with forensic, psychological, and behavioral inconsistencies tracking with a stark image depicting a dissociative state. The physical fluidity of characteristics, the contradictory statements, and the diary entries documenting the internal conflict between the two personas only serve to further this claim, leaving little room for doubt that Eve had taken full control.

The Evidence
The detectives of this case was so invested in finding out the truth that they noticed even the slightest details of their interviews with Hannah or “Eve”, and here are some of the main points that made them believe that this was a severe case of MPD:

  • The Bruise Mystery: In one recorded interview, Hannah appeared with a bruise, yet Eve—interviewed later—showed no sign of it. Additionally, Eve seemed uncertain about which side of the face had been bruised, suggesting a lapse in memory between identities. An interesting detail to note is that Eve prefers black coffee, while Hannah likes sweet tea.

  • Fingerprint Puzzle: Fingerprints found at a crucial location did not match Hannah’s; instead, they appeared to belong to an unidentified individual. At first glance, this implicated two different people, but forensic experts speculated that it could have been one person trying to conceal her identity, not two different people at the scene.

  • The Impossible Alibi: The most puzzling part of the case was that Hannah had an "alibi" of being in Glasgow at the supposed time of the murder. Of course, it is not humanly possible for one to exist in two locations at once. It later emerged that Hannah had altered Simon's watch, adjusting the time of death, before she left for Glasgow.
The Diary Entries: A Disturbing Confession
Most incriminating, however, was evidence uncovered on Hannah's and Eve's private diary entries, secreted away on their premises. The diaries exposed Hannah's and Eve's private interactions with each other, recording the dynamic changes at play as their relationship tore back and forth, and documenting their desperate attempts at keeping up their image. These journals provide a chilling description of a broken mind's attempt at constructing a semblance of normalcy:

| June 16, 1994 (Hannah) – “Eve... I'm sorry... I don’t know what came over me. I really didn’t mean to kill him... you know how unstable I am... You’ll have to take over... report him as missing to the police... cry a little, but not too much. You’ve always been better at acting... I’m leaving this to you.”

| June 18, 1994 (Eve) – “You’re a mess, Hannah. But fine. I did it. I played the role of a dutiful wife, dabbed my eyes, said that Simon vanished when we were in Glasgow. Cops bought it. The next interview is your turn. Practice the answers. And for god’s sake, don’t flinch when they mention the mirror.”

| June 25, 1994 (Hannah) – “I told them about the arguments. They noticed the bruises. I lied. I told them about Eric and Diane. I can’t do this… You’ll have to take over. They might catch on to us. Hide the diary.”

| June 27, 1994 (Eve) – “I told them, just like we planned. Told them we weren’t in the house all of Friday night. Said we were in Glasgow. Acted feeble. They asked about the mirror too. I just told them it’s upstairs.”

| June 30, 1994 (Hannah) – “They’re onto us… I forgot about the guitar… they also found the wig… I almost… never mind. Just don’t mess up. I’m counting on you.”

| July 1, 1994 (Eve) – “Idiot. How the fuck did you forget about it? We’re safe for now. And fix the coffee thing—You drink it black now, not tea. They noticed.”

| July 2, 1994 (Hannah) – “They’re really onto us now… they think we’re twins. The Glasgow alibi isn’t holding up. Sorry… I panicked. I’m really sorry… please don’t do this to me… no…” "Goodbye Hannah-"

| July 3, 1994 (Eve) – "I'm sorry Simon.. if only I had taken control sooner… I hope you’ll be okay, Thank you for everything.. it's all over now."

In her last diary entry, Eve confesses that she had finally taken complete control, banishing Hannah’s control, with a deep regret for not having taken control of her body earlier.

What Comes Next?

Hannah's case has also raised a passionate controversy regarding her guilt for the offense—does all blame lie on her, or does her mental condition have much bearing on the issue at hand? Legal pundits suggest that the case can be analyzed according to the M’Naghten Rule, a legal criterion that checks if a person knew what they were doing at the time they committed a criminal offense.

Dissociative Identity Disorder has previously been used as a defense, but remains controversial. Research, as published in The Bulletin of the American Academy of Psychiatry and the Law, indicated that fewer than 1% of defendants have attempted a plea of insanity, with only 28% of those succeeding. The courts, more frequently than not, have rejected such claims, sometimes ruling they were not real but feigned.

If Hannah or Eve is ruled not guilty due to insanity, she will be hospitalized at a psychiatric facility, where her ongoing evaluation will determine whether she still qualified for extended hospitalization. Alternatively, if she is convicted, she may face a lifetime prison term with no parole.

As the trial progresses, one cannot help but ask: was this a premeditated act of false pretence, or a desperate breakdown of a mind divided against itself?
